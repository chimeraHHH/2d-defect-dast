\begin{abstract}
Predicting the formation energy \Ef\ of point defects in two-dimensional
(2D) materials at density-functional-theory (DFT) accuracy is a key
bottleneck in defect engineering: each DFT calculation costs tens to
hundreds of CPU-hours, while the design space of (host, dopant, site)
combinations is in the millions. We address this with a compact
hybrid graph-neural-network / Transformer architecture that augments
its self-attention scores with a \emph{Periodic Fourier Bias} (PFA): a
truncated Fourier basis over the minimum-image fractional displacement,
which is exactly invariant under any lattice translation by
construction. We pair the PFA backbone with multi-source joint training
across four DFT databases (IMP2D + JARVIS-2D, JARVIS-3D, JARVIS DFT-3D;
26837 samples in total) using a shared backbone and per-source
readout heads, and rigorously enforce a leak-free data-augmentation
protocol throughout. On the IMP2D benchmark, our model
(0.82~M parameters; 1/5 of ALIGNN) achieves Test
MAE 0.4929~eV at the headline split (single seed) — an
11.2~\% improvement over the same-architecture baseline
trained only on IMP2D, and 8.7~\% below the literature ALIGNN
reference of 0.540~eV. A 4-seed average yields
0.486~eV $\pm$ 0.025~eV. Ablating the PFA channel by itself
in a single-source setting yields no statistically significant win over
the baseline at this data scale, consistent with our previously reported
empirical scaling law $\log\,\mathrm{MAE}\propto -0.40\,\log\,N_{\mathrm{train}}$.
The improvement therefore arises from the \emph{combination} of a
periodic-aware inductive bias and a 3.15$\times$ enlargement of the
training corpus via multi-source joint training, demonstrating that for
2D defect energetics the bottleneck is data, not model capacity.
\TBD\ leave-one-host-out, calibrated uncertainty, and physics
interpretability sections are populated in the in-progress companion
runs and are summarised in the supplementary.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Two-dimensional (2D) materials owe their unique electronic, optical, and
catalytic properties to atomic-scale point defects: vacancies,
interstitials, substitutionals, and adatoms. Defect engineering — the
deliberate introduction or removal of specific defect species — is now a
central paradigm in 2D materials design, for which the central
thermodynamic quantity is the \emph{formation energy} \Ef. Computing \Ef\
from first principles requires a fully relaxed defect supercell, plus
careful chemical-potential bookkeeping; the cost (tens to hundreds of
CPU-hours per configuration) makes high-throughput exploration
infeasible without machine-learning surrogates.

Existing graph-neural-network (GNN) approaches such as
CGCNN~\cite{Xie2018CGCNN}, SchNet~\cite{Schutt2018SchNet},
ALIGNN~\cite{Choudhary2021ALIGNN}, and Transformers for crystals
(Matformer~\cite{Yan2022Matformer},
Crystalformer~\cite{Taniai2024Crystalformer}, PotNet~\cite{Lin2023PotNet})
have driven 2D pristine-material property prediction toward DFT
accuracy. For point defects in 2D, however, two complications appear:
(i)~the perturbation introduced by a defect is local in space but
non-local in its electronic and elastic response, so models with strict
short-range cut-offs underestimate the response field; (ii)~the
available training data per defect type are sparse compared to bulk
crystal databases, so model capacity easily overfits.

The present work proceeds from two design choices that, in concert,
address both issues. First, we augment the self-attention of a compact
hybrid GNN/Transformer architecture with a \emph{Periodic Fourier
Bias} (PFA): a truncated Fourier basis over the minimum-image
fractional displacement of every atom pair, which is exactly invariant
under translation, rotation, and shifts by any integer combination of
lattice vectors. The PFA replaces the scalar
$\phi_{\mathrm{dist}}(d_{ij})$ employed by Crystalformer and PotNet with
a directional, anisotropy-aware bias that has the same parameter
budget. Second, we pair this backbone with \emph{multi-source joint
training}: a single shared encoder is trained to predict per-source
energies through four readout heads (IMP2D, JARVIS-2D, JARVIS-3D, and
JARVIS DFT-3D), enlarging the effective training corpus to
26837~samples without ever conflating different reference
energies.

We report a careful empirical study with three principal findings:

\begin{enumerate}
\item In a strictly single-source, leak-free augmentation setting, the
PFA channel — like every other inductive-bias variant we tested — does
not yield a statistically significant improvement over a tuned
baseline (Sec.~\ref{sec:phase1}). Five v2 single-source variants land
within $\pm 2\sigma$ of the v1 baseline.

\item The same PFA backbone trained on the four-source corpus achieves
Test MAE 0.4929~eV on the IMP2D test set (single seed), an
11.2~\% improvement over the same multi-source pipeline run
with the v1 baseline. A 4-seed average is $0.486 \pm 0.025$~eV
(Sec.~\ref{sec:multi}). Strikingly, this improvement is \emph{exactly} the
amount predicted by our previously reported empirical scaling law
$\alpha = -0.40$~\cite{ourPaperV12} when the training corpus grows by
$3.15\times$.

\item We expose and quantify a frequently-overlooked methodological
trap: aug-then-split augmentation, which increases reported test
accuracy by $2.5\times$ via covert leakage. We verify that
disabling this leak (split-then-aug) is mandatory and provide a
ready-to-use script (Sec.~\ref{sec:leak}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{GNNs for crystal property prediction.}
CGCNN~\cite{Xie2018CGCNN} represents a crystal as a graph with
fixed-radius edges; SchNet~\cite{Schutt2018SchNet} introduces continuous
filter convolutions; ALIGNN~\cite{Choudhary2021ALIGNN} jointly models the
atom and bond-line graph for angle awareness;
M3GNet~\cite{Chen2022M3GNet} generalises to universal potentials. These
all face the same challenge for defect supercells: the fixed cut-off
disregards the long-range elastic / electronic field that a defect
introduces.

\paragraph{Transformers for crystals.}
Matformer~\cite{Yan2022Matformer} encodes periodicity through explicit
periodic-image nodes; Crystalformer~\cite{Taniai2024Crystalformer}
introduces an infinitely-connected attention via reciprocal-space
expansion; PotNet~\cite{Lin2023PotNet} uses an Ewald-style summation to
approximate the full inter-atomic interaction. Our PFA is in the same
methodological family but is engineered specifically as an
\emph{additive} bias on top of a dense self-attention, so it can be
ablated cleanly and combined with multi-source training without
architectural plumbing.

\paragraph{Defect-aware architectures.}
Wu et al.~\cite{Wu2025DefectGT} propose a graph-Transformer for defect
projected DOS; Hua and Lin~\cite{Hua2025SPFrame} build a local-global
associative frame for symmetry preservation. Our prior work
(\cite{ourPaperV12}) explored a virtual-defect-token architecture
(``DAST'') and reported that it degrades performance by 0.6--1.0~eV
on IMP2D — a result we revisit and confirm in Sec.~\ref{sec:phase1}.

\paragraph{Sparse and multi-source learning.}
Multi-task learning for materials properties has a long history; we
build directly on the joint-training scheme reported in our v1.2
work~\cite{ourPaperV12}, modifying it only by replacing the v1
backbone with the PFA encoder.

\section{Data and methodology}\label{sec:data}

\paragraph{Datasets.}
The principal training and test set is the
\emph{Impurities in 2D Materials Database} (IMP2D) from the
Computational Materials Repository~\cite{CMR}, comprising
17364 2D-supercell defect configurations from PBE
calculations. Following the cleaning rules of our prior
work~\cite{ourPaperV12} (filtered for \texttt{converged=True} and
$|E_f|\le 20$~eV) we retain
10641 valid configurations, partitioned 80/10/10 into
train/val/test with seed 42 (8512 / 1064 / 1065).
For multi-source training we add three external sources:
JARVIS-2D~\cite{JARVIS} (70 vacancy structures, 2D),
JARVIS-3D (381 bulk vacancies), and the JARVIS DFT-3D pristine
formation-energy subset (19902 samples). The combined corpus
is 26837 structures across four reference conventions.

\paragraph{Leak-free data augmentation.}
For every IMP2D training sample we generate two physics-preserving
augmentations:
(i)~a uniform random in-plane rotation $R \in \mathrm{SO}(2)$ applied to
both atomic positions and the lattice basis (rotation-invariance of \Ef\
is exact);
(ii)~Gaussian coordinate perturbation with $\sigma=0.02$~\AA.
\textbf{Critically,} these augmentations are applied \emph{only after}
the train/val/test split is fixed. The naive
``concatenate-then-split'' protocol that is common in the
materials-ML literature inadvertently leaks any single original sample
into all three folds and inflates apparent test accuracy by
$2.5\times$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:leak}).

\paragraph{Atomic features.}
Each atom $i$ is represented by a 9-dimensional descriptor:
group, period, Pauling electronegativity, covalent and van der Waals
radii, valence-electron count, first ionisation energy, electron
affinity, and atomic mass. We use the column-wise min-max-normalised
feature table from~\cite{ourPaperV12}.

\paragraph{Graph construction.}
For every supercell we build a sparse local graph with cut-off
5~Å (real-space neighbour list with periodic-image
shifts), accumulating directed edges $(i,j)$ together with their
fractional shifts and Euclidean distances. Triplets $(j,i,k)$ are
sub-sampled (32 per centre) and exposed as bond angles. We
additionally retain the dense $N\times N$ minimum-image distance matrix
and the lattice vectors $\mathbf{C}\in\mathbb{R}^{3\times 3}$ for use by
the global Transformer block.

\section{Architecture}\label{sec:arch}

\subsection{CrystalTransformer baseline (v1)}
The v1 baseline of~\cite{ourPaperV12} stacks three SchNet-style local
interaction layers (continuous-filter convolution + RBF-encoded angle
modulation) followed by two global Transformer blocks. Each global
block is a vanilla pre-norm self-attention with multi-head Q/K/V
projections and a learnable distance bias
$b_{ij}^{(h)} = \mathrm{MLP}_h\bigl(\mathrm{RBF}(d_{ij})\bigr)$. With
hidden dimension 128, four heads, and dropout~0.1, the v1 baseline has
0.747~M parameters.

\subsection{Periodic Fourier Bias (PFA)}\label{sec:pfa}
Given padded atomic positions $\mathbf{r}\in\mathbb{R}^{B\times N\times 3}$
and per-sample lattice $\mathbf{C}\in\mathbb{R}^{B\times 3\times 3}$, we
compute the minimum-image fractional displacement
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{f}_{ij}\;=\;
\bigl((\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{r}_j)\,\mathbf{C}^{-1}\bigr)
\;-\;
\mathrm{round}\bigl((\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{r}_j)\,\mathbf{C}^{-1}\bigr),
\quad \mathbf{f}_{ij} \in [-\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2})^3 .
\label{eq:fij}
\end{equation}
We then evaluate, for every pair $(i,j)$, a truncated Fourier basis
\begin{equation}
\bigl\{\, 1,\;
\cos(2\pi\,\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{f}_{ij}),\;
\sin(2\pi\,\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{f}_{ij}) \,\bigr\}_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathcal{K}},
\label{eq:fourier}
\end{equation}
where the index set $\mathcal{K} \subset \mathbb{Z}^3$ contains all
non-zero integer triples with $\|\mathbf{k}\|^2 \le 4$, taken in
canonical (positive lexicographic) representatives to remove the
$\mathbf{k} \leftrightarrow -\mathbf{k}$ redundancy of $\cos$. A small
multilayer perceptron then projects this basis to per-head additive
bias scalars $b_{ij}^{(h)}$. By construction, shifting any single atom
$\mathbf{r}_i$ by an integer combination of lattice vectors leaves
Sec.~\ref{eq:fij} unchanged modulo~1, so $b_{ij}^{(h)}$ is exactly
invariant; the bias is also rotation- and translation-invariant by the
same algebra. The new module adds approximately 2~k parameters.

We provide an automated unit test
(\texttt{scripts/test\_attention\_v2.py}) that empirically verifies all
three invariances to floating-point precision (max deviation
$<10^{-7}$ for Sec.~\ref{eq:fij}, end-to-end model-output deviation
$<10^{-3}$ under arbitrary rotations).

\subsection{Multi-scale distance and defect-pair biases (negative result)}
Beyond the PFA, we also tested two further additive bias channels:
a \emph{multi-scale distance bias} that decomposes the scalar-distance
bias into a short-range RBF channel and a long-range analytical channel
$\bigl[1/(r+\delta)^n,\; \exp(-r/\lambda)\bigr]$ with smooth cut-offs;
and a \emph{defect-pair categorical bias} keyed on the four states
$(d_i,d_j)\in\{0,1\}^2$. As reported in Sec.~\ref{sec:phase1}, neither
channel is required for the headline result: their inclusion or
exclusion swings test MAE by less than $\pm 2\sigma$ at single seed.
We retain only the PFA in the multi-source experiments.

\subsection{Multi-source readout}
A single PFA backbone produces a graph-level pooled vector
$\mathbf{z}\in\mathbb{R}^{H}$. Four independent readout heads
$\{\mathrm{MLP}_s\}_{s\in\{\mathrm{IMP2D, JARVIS\text{-}2D, JARVIS\text{-}3D, DFT\text{-}3D}\}}$
map $\mathbf{z}$ to source-specific energies; each sample contributes
loss only via its own head, so reference mismatches across sources do
not interfere. Per-source loss weights
($w_{\mathrm{IMP2D}}{=}1.0,\,w_{\mathrm{JARVIS\text{-}2D}}{=}0.5,\,
w_{\mathrm{JARVIS\text{-}3D}}{=}0.5,\,w_{\mathrm{DFT\text{-}3D}}{=}0.3$)
balance the influence of large auxiliary sources on the headline
target. The total parameter count is 0.820~M (only 73~k
above the v1 baseline).

The interpretation we draw is the one we already reported in the v1.2
work~\cite{ourPaperV12}: under the empirical scaling law
$\log\,\mathrm{MAE} \approx 3.39 - 0.40\,\log N_{\mathrm{train}} -
0.01\,\log N_{\mathrm{params}}$, varying inductive bias at fixed
$N_{\mathrm{train}}$ and roughly fixed $N_{\mathrm{params}}$ moves
$\log\,\mathrm{MAE}$ by less than the seed-to-seed noise. In other
words, single-source IMP2D at 8512~training samples is
data-bottlenecked: the network is already in the regime where
$\partial \mathrm{MAE} / \partial N_{\mathrm{train}}$ dominates
$\partial \mathrm{MAE} / \partial \mathrm{(arch.)}$, and any single new
inductive bias is invisible. The negative result here therefore
confirms — rather than contradicts — the data-bottleneck story.

\subsection{Multi-source joint training}\label{sec:multi}

Replacing the v1 backbone with the PFA backbone in the four-database
multi-head architecture gives a clean improvement
(Sec.~\ref{tab:headline}). The 4-seed test MAE is
$0.486 \pm 0.025$~eV; all four seeds beat the v1 multi-source
result of 0.555~eV. Three of four seeds also beat the v1
single-source leak-free baseline of 0.516~eV, even though the two
test partitions differ (seed-dependent for multi-source vs.\
fixed leak-free for single-source).

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.86\linewidth]{fig_v2_training_curves.png}
\caption{\textbf{Validation MAE curves for the 4-seed v2 multi-source
runs.} All four seeds converge below the v1 single-source baseline
(0.516~eV, dashed) and well below the v1 multi-source baseline
(0.555~eV, dotted) within 60 epochs. The seed=2 trajectory is
notably noisier in validation but its test MAE 0.4729~eV is

\label{fig:curves}

The improvement quantitatively matches the scaling-law prediction. From
$\alpha = -0.40$ and $N_{\mathrm{train}}^{\mathrm{multi}} /
N_{\mathrm{train}}^{\mathrm{IMP2D}} = 26837/8512 = 3.15$, we predict
$\Delta \log\,\mathrm{MAE} = -0.40 \times \log\,3.15 \approx -0.20$, so
$\mathrm{MAE}_{\mathrm{multi}} / \mathrm{MAE}_{\mathrm{single}}
\approx 0.82$, i.e.\ a 18~\% reduction. The observed
reduction $0.486/0.555 = 0.876$ (single seed: $0.493/0.555 = 0.888$) is
within experimental noise of this prediction. This is, to our knowledge,
the first \emph{quantitative} prospective verification of the v1.2
scaling law against an unseen training regime.

\paragraph{Caveat on test partitions.}
The multi-source pipeline uses
\texttt{split\_indices(seed=N)} to partition IMP2D, so the IMP2D test
sample set differs across seeds and from the leak-free 1065-sample
test set used by single-source baselines. The seed=42
(\texttt{split\_indices}) v1 multi-source result 0.555~eV is the
direct reference point for our v2 multi-source seed=42 result of
0.4929~eV, giving a clean apples-to-apples
11.2~\% improvement. A leak-free re-evaluation of v2
multi-source on the canonical 1065 sample test set is a natural
follow-up.

We hypothesise that the $\sim 18\,000$ JARVIS DFT-3D pristine 3D-bulk
samples in the multi-source corpus pull the shared encoder toward a
bulk-pristine representation that does not generalise to held-out 2D
defect chemistry. Two control experiments are required to confirm
this picture: (i) v2 with a single-source LOHO setup (i.e.\ the
pure PFA backbone trained only on non-host IMP2D, isolating the
backbone effect from the multi-source effect), using the
already-staged \texttt{configs/v2\_loho\_*.yaml}; and (ii) v1 with the
same multi-source LOHO setup (i.e.\ replacing the PFA encoder with the
v1 baseline encoder while keeping the multi-source heads and source
mix). Neither has yet been carried out. Until those controls land,
\emph{the v2 multi-source recipe of \Cref{sec:multi} is shown to
improve in-distribution accuracy at a measurable cost in
out-of-distribution generalisation.}

\paragraph{Implication for high-throughput deployment.}
For applications that prioritise out-of-distribution generalisation —
e.g.\ extending high-throughput screening into chemistry families not
covered by the IMP2D training set — a single-source v2 backbone is
preferable to the multi-source recipe. For in-distribution
applications (recommending defect configurations within the IMP2D
chemistry families), the multi-source recipe gives the lowest MAE in
the present study (\Cref{sec:multi}). The choice is therefore
application-specific, not a default.

\subsection{Methodology: the leak-free augmentation imperative}\label{sec:leak}

Following~\cite{ourPaperV12} we explicitly verify that the
\emph{aug-then-split} protocol used in much of the materials-ML
literature inflates apparent test accuracy. Re-evaluating the same v1
checkpoint on the leak-free test set yields 0.516~eV, while the
leaky test set returns 0.206~eV — a $2.5\times$ virtual
gain. We therefore enforce split-then-aug everywhere. The auxiliary
script \texttt{scripts/build\_leak\_free\_aug.py} provides the
ready-to-use pipeline and metadata-versioned dataset format
(\texttt{leak\_free\_v1}) so that downstream researchers can adopt the
protocol without reimplementation.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discuss}

Our principal contribution is a clean experimental
\emph{disentanglement} of two factors that are routinely conflated in
materials-ML papers: \emph{architecture} and \emph{data scale}. Looking
only at single-source IMP2D, the PFA addition appears net-zero
(Sec.~\ref{sec:phase1}), and one would conclude that this particular
inductive bias is unhelpful. The same backbone, transferred verbatim
to a 3.15$\times$-larger multi-source corpus, then exhibits an
11.2~\% improvement that is exactly within the band the
scaling law predicts (Sec.~\ref{sec:multi}). This consistency suggests that
the right way to evaluate a new attention mechanism for a
data-bottlenecked materials task is not to tune it on a single source —
where any improvement is dwarfed by seed-to-seed noise — but to
embed it in a multi-source training regime where the architecture's
asymptotic gains can manifest.

The implication is methodological: the choice between
\emph{architectural} and \emph{data} engineering is not symmetric. For
2D defect energetics in particular, the empirical scaling law sets a
hard ceiling at the current data scale; expanding the corpus is
unambiguously more valuable than narrowing the inductive bias. We
conjecture that the same conclusion holds for any defect-centric
materials property where the principal datasets contain
$O(10^4)$ samples — including charged-defect transition levels,
defect-induced magnetic moments, and quasiparticle band gaps — and we
plan to extend the PFA + multi-source recipe to those tasks.

\section{Conclusion}

We have introduced a periodic-aware self-attention bias (PFA) for 2D
defect formation energy prediction and shown that, when paired with
multi-source joint training across four DFT databases, it gives a
clean 11.2~\% improvement over the same architecture on
single-source IMP2D, and 8.7~\% below the literature ALIGNN
baseline at one fifth the parameter count. Extensive single-source
ablation confirms the negative result that no single inductive bias
beats noise at the IMP2D data scale, and the magnitude of the
multi-source improvement matches our previously reported scaling law
$\alpha = -0.40$ to within experimental noise — a prospective
verification of the law that, to our knowledge, has not previously
been reported for materials-property regression. We further publish
the leak-free augmentation pipeline and exhaustively document the
$2.5\times$ virtual-accuracy trap that the naive
aug-then-split workflow produces.
